Blood in Stool with IBD: How to Track It and When It Is Urgent

Posted on July 15, 2026 · Flares

Content note: Educational content aligned with publicly available patient materials from the Crohn's & Colitis Foundation and other major IBD education sources. IBDPal is not affiliated with or endorsed by the Foundation. Last reviewed June 2026. Not individual medical advice.

Educational use only. IBDPal does not provide medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Always consult your gastroenterologist or IBD care team for personal decisions.

Seeing blood with a bowel movement is frightening, even for people who have lived with ulcerative colitis or Crohn's colitis for years. Not every speck is an emergency, and not every little bit is safe to ignore. The goal is to describe bleeding accurately, know urgent patterns, and contact the right level of care.

Describe amount, color, and timing

Clinicians need specifics, not only that there was blood. Note whether you see bright red blood on the toilet paper, streaks on the stool surface, blood mixed through the stool, maroon color, or black tarry stools. Estimate volume in practical terms: streaks, teaspoon, tablespoon, or enough to turn the toilet water red and require multiple pads. Record whether bleeding happens with every stool, only with hard stools, or between stools.

  • Color: bright red, dark red, maroon, black/tarry
  • Amount: streaks vs soaking pads or clots
  • Associated symptoms: dizziness, pain, fever, rapid heartbeat
  • Medicines: steroids, biologics, anticoagulants, NSAIDs, iron

Patterns that still need a prompt GI call

New or increasing rectal bleeding, even if modest, should be reported to your gastroenterology team, especially if you also have urgency, night stools, or a rising flare pattern. Your nurse line may arrange labs, stool studies, steroid guidance, or an urgent clinic visit. Pair symptom notes with flare first 48 hours steps while you wait for a response.

When bleeding becomes urgent

Seek emergency care for heavy bleeding, large clots, black stools with lightheadedness, fainting, confusion, chest pain, severe abdominal pain, or inability to keep fluids down. People on blood thinners or with low baseline hemoglobin should have a low threshold to go in. See when to go to the ER with IBD and GI nurse line vs ER.

Other causes your team may consider

Hemorrhoids, anal fissures, polyps, infection, and medication effects can cause or worsen bleeding. Do not assume every bleed is just IBD, and do not assume hemorrhoids explain everything without evaluation. Colonoscopy timing, imaging, and labs are individualized.

What not to do while you wait

Avoid NSAIDs like ibuprofen unless your clinician has approved them. Do not start over-the-counter anti-diarrheals for bloody diarrhea without advice. Keep sipping fluids if you can, and lie down if you feel faint. Bring a photo of the stool only if your clinic asks; many teams prefer verbal volume descriptions.

Preparing for the visit or ER

List your last hemoglobin, last scope findings, and current IBD medicines including the date of your last biologic infusion or injection. Mention prior transfusions or hospitalizations for bleeding. Clear information speeds safer decisions.

Educational materials from CCF stress that rectal bleeding deserves clinician input and that heavy bleeding is an emergency. Trust your body's warning signs over internet reassurance.
